Rift Valley Fever Poster

Rift Valley Fever (RVF) is an acute, fever-causing viral disease most commonly observed in domesticated animals with the ability to infect and cause illness in humans. RVF is generally found in regions of eastern and southern Africa where sheep and cattle are raised, but the virus exists in most of sub-Saharan Africa, including west Africa and Madagascar. There have also been outbreak reports in Saudi Arabia and subsequently, Yemen.

These posters were designed to help prevent RVF and provide guidance if animals and/or people contract RVF.

In order to make the document was accessible I covered all of the elements of accessibility in design. Since color contrast is important for readability we made the two primary colors darker. Then I went through the document in InDesign and properly tagged the document.

Once the first poster was accessible, I did the same process for the next two English posters and then the French versions.

Branding Templates

The Graphics Branch of the CDC can output, on average, around 6500 graphic projects in a year. A large percentage of those are Fact Sheets which are 1-2 page documents covering a specific topic. In order to keep up with the demand, templates were created to speed up the design process as well as standardize the CDC branding.

This project updated the design of the templates as well as made them accessible.

To the right (top) is an example of one of the older Fact Sheet templates. There are several brances of the CDC and each one has their own color branding. This orange is the primary color for the National Center for Emerging and Zoonotic Infectious Diseases (NCEZID). For the redesign of the Fact Sheets, the end goal was something that would combine the indivuality of the CDC branches with the visual brand of the CDC.

For the final result the title bar had the primary branch color in different shades that all had enough color contrast to be accessible. The rays in the title bar were mimicing the rays in the CDC logo to further visualize the CDC branding of the product. The footer bar also contained all of the CDC branch’s colors. In addition, the InDesign document was given all of the necessary settings to make designing an accessible document a breeze.

Home Care Task Redesign

Medical Information Technology, or MEDITECH, is a medical electronic health record (EHR) company focused on making the jobs of doctors and nurses easier by digitizing medical processes and records for ease of use. During my time working this company, I worked on thier Home Care software which is a task-based system focused on patients needing home health and hospice care. Easily the largest design I lead while there was an enhancement to the Manage OASIS Assessments (MOA) task.

OASIS is an acronym that means Outcome and Assessment Information Set. This is a patient- specific, standardized assessment used in Medicare home health care to plan care, determine reimbursement, and measure quality of treatment. The MOA task in Home Care was designed to manage all of the patient assessments conducted by teams of field nurses. The enhancement saw a variety of updates that made the task and others involving its use more user friendly as well as provide additional functionality.

A communication system was established so the reviewers and field nurses could discuss the assessments. The ability to print the conversation was added as well as a report to view the discussion. A status system was created to properly inform where the assessment was in the review process. Fixing assessment issues was streamlined. And other related tasks were updated to include the new status information.

Zika Emergency Response Accessibility

Zika is a virus that is primarily spread through infected mosquito bites, but can also be transmitted sexually. This virus can also be spread from pregnant women to their babies which can cause birth defects.

I was hired at the CDC during their emergency response to this virus. Many graphics were created as quickly as possible to spread vital information to the public, but these graphics weren’t created to be accessible. While the accessible information could be found online, it was still necessary to remake the graphic PDFs in an accessible format. I had a key role in making sure that these graphics were accessible and complied with both Section 508 and the Department on Health and Human Services regulations.
